The little Northern phrasebook

  • Moin The Northern greeting – used at ANY time of day, not just in the morning. In use: “Moin!” – even at 3 pm.
  • Moin Moin The doubled version. To some Northerners it already counts as chatter – one “Moin” is plenty.
  • Schietwetter Bad, cold, wet weather. “Schiet” means muck – so the weather is rubbish. In use: “In this Schietwetter we’re staying in.”
  • Schmuddelwetter Grey, damp, dreary – the quieter cousin of Schietwetter.
  • Klönschnack A cosy, relaxed chat – often over the garden fence or at the harbour. In use: “We had a little Klönschnack.”
  • schnacken to talk, to chat. “Platt schnacken” means to speak Low German.
  • Deern A girl or young woman – always affectionate, never derogatory. In use: “Well, my Deern?”
  • Jung / Jung' A boy or young lad – the male counterpart to Deern.
  • Feudel The floor cloth. “feudeln” = to mop the floor. In use: “I still need to feudeln.”
  • Buddel A bottle. A “Buddelschiff” is a ship in a bottle.
  • Lütt little. A “Lütt un Lütt” is a small beer with a schnapps. In use: “A little one, please.”
  • Fofftein A break – literally “fifteen” (minutes). “Fofftein maken” = to take a break.
  • tüddelig / vertüddelt confused, muddled. To get “vertüddelt” is to get tangled up. In use: “I’m all in a muddle.”
  • Puschen Slippers.
  • sabbeln to chatter, to prattle. “Sabbel” can also mean the mouth.
  • dröge dry – of the weather and of humour (“dröger Humor”).
  • Muckefuck Weak or ersatz coffee – the “Plörre” of hot drinks.
  • Plörre A weak, watery drink – usually bad coffee.
  • betüddeln / betütern to fuss over someone, to coddle.
  • nölen to whine, grumble, dawdle.
  • Kladderadatsch a big mess, chaos – often with a crash.
  • angeschickert tipsy, slightly drunk.
  • kabbelig Of the sea: choppy, short steep waves – a “kabbelige See”.
  • achtern at the back. Nautical: “nach achtern” = towards the stern.
  • Steert the (rear) end, the tail – in sailors’ talk also a rope’s end.
  • pütschern to potter about, or to splash around in the water.
  • Nordlicht Jokingly: a Northern German – not only the aurora.
  • Hummel Hummel A Hamburg call; the correct reply is “Mors Mors”. Known with a wink around the Baltic too.

verified on 10 July 2026 Source: common North German and Low German usage (regional)

Common questions about the Northern way of speaking

What does “Moin” mean?

“Moin” is the North German greeting – used at any time of day, not just in the morning. It probably comes from “moi” (nice/good), not from “Morgen” (morning).

Is “Moin” only said in the morning?

No. “Moin” works all day – morning, noon and night. That’s exactly what surprises newcomers.

What is Plattdeutsch?

Plattdeutsch (Low German) is the traditional regional language of North Germany. Many everyday words like “schnacken”, “Deern” or “Feudel” come from it.

What does “Schietwetter” mean?

Bad, cold, wet weather. “Schiet” means muck – a word used often on the coast.
